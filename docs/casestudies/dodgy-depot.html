Dodgy Depot

A retrofuturistic first-person job simulation game set in a world where work never ends.

Overview

Dodgy Depot is a first-person job simulation game with a retrofuturistic aesthetic. Players navigate a strange, bureaucratic workplace where the mundane becomes surreal. The project gave me full ownership of the narrative and audio direction — working largely independently from concept to delivery.

My Contribution
  • Developed the full cast of 30+ characters with distinct voices, roles, and dialogue arcs
  • Designed and wrote the complete dialogue system — branching conversations, in-world emails, tutorial text
  • Built the worldbuilding from scratch: lore, internal glossary, invented slang (motherclanker, Vorraxian gibberish), company history
  • Directed the audio and music — source selection, implementation logic, ambient design
  • Created easter eggs and cross-references woven throughout the environment

"The narrative layer was consistently cited by playtesters and peers as the element that made Dodgy Depot feel like a real place rather than a game level."

Narrative Design Approach

The core challenge was building a world that felt lived-in without overwhelming the player with lore. I approached it through layering: essential story beats in dialogue, background texture in environmental details, deeper lore accessible only to players who looked for it.

Character voices were the most demanding part. With 30+ NPCs sharing the same workspace, each needed a distinct register — from the aggressively upbeat receptionist to the quietly nihilistic warehouse foreman — without any feeling like a parody.

Sound Direction

Sound design doubled as narrative design in Dodgy Depot. The sonic palette — muffled machinery, degraded muzak, intermittent PA announcements — was built to reinforce the sense of a system running slightly past its expiry date. Each audio layer was chosen to add atmosphere without explaining it.

What I Learned

Managing the full narrative pipeline solo taught me how to prioritise ruthlessly. Not every character needs a deep arc. Not every piece of lore needs to be findable. The world feels complete when the parts the player does see feel inevitable — not when everything is documented.

Friction with the production side of the team also sharpened my instinct for defending narrative decisions with design language rather than creative intuition alone.
