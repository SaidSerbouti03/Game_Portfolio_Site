Dodgy Depot

A retrofuturistic first-person job simulation game set in a world where work never ends.

Overview

Dodgy Depot is a first-person job simulation game with a retrofuturistic aesthetic. Players navigate a strange, bureaucratic workplace where the mundane becomes surreal. The project gave me full ownership of the narrative and audio direction — working largely independently from concept to delivery.

My Contribution
  • Developed the full cast of 30+ characters with distinct voices, roles, and dialogue arcs
  • Designed and wrote the complete dialogue system — branching conversations, in-world emails, tutorial text
  • Built the worldbuilding from scratch: lore, internal glossary, invented slang, company history
  • Directed the audio and music — source selection, implementation logic, ambient design
  • Created easter eggs and cross-references woven throughout the environment

"The narrative layer was consistently cited by playtesters and peers as the element that made Dodgy Depot feel like a real place rather than a game level."

Narrative Design Approach

The core challenge was building a world that felt lived-in without overwhelming the player with lore. I approached it through layering: essential story beats in dialogue, background texture in environmental details, deeper lore accessible only to players who looked for it.

Character voices were the most demanding part. With 30+ NPCs sharing the same workspace, each needed a distinct register — from the aggressively upbeat receptionist to the quietly nihilistic warehouse foreman — without any feeling like a parody.

Sound Direction

Sound design doubled as narrative design in Dodgy Depot. The sonic palette — muffled machinery, degraded muzak, intermittent PA announcements — was built to reinforce the sense of a system running slightly past its expiry date. Each audio layer was chosen to add atmosphere without explaining it.

What I Learned

Managing the full narrative pipeline solo taught me how to prioritise ruthlessly. Not every character needs a deep arc. Not every piece of lore needs to be findable. The world feels complete when the parts the player does see feel inevitable — not when everything is documented.

Friction with the production side of the team also sharpened my instinct for defending narrative decisions with design language rather than creative intuition alone.

Dream Jumper

A first-person liminal horror walking simulator with roguelite elements. You fall asleep. The spaces remember.

Concept

Dream Jumper follows Elias, a young man trapped in recursive dreamscapes — familiar spaces warped just past recognition. Each run generates a new configuration of liminal rooms pulled from his own memory, each holding fragments of the life he's trying to piece together.

The game sits between horror and melancholy. The threat is never a monster — it's the creeping sense that the architecture knows you.

Documents Produced
  • Game Design Document v3.0 — full systemic and narrative specification
  • Level Design Document: Silent Pool — atmospheric horror, memory as space
  • Level Design Document: Playdate — domestic uncanny, childhood distorted
  • Pitch deck for internal presentation
  • AI image prompt documentation for visual direction

"Both level design documents were praised for their original mechanical thinking — the narrative and spatial logic working as a single system rather than two parallel tracks."

Narrative Design Approach

The central design constraint was that space must carry story. Dream Jumper has no cutscenes, minimal dialogue, and no explicit exposition. Every piece of information a player receives comes through environmental detail, audio cues, or the behaviour of the space itself.

This meant designing levels as narrative instruments first, playable spaces second — then finding the intersection.

Ostrava: The Old Quarter

A level design exercise — six zones, one city, a spatial logic that tells its own story.

Overview

An academic level design presentation built around a fictional district of Ostrava, Czech Republic. The brief was to design a space with contextual logic — each zone had to feel like it belonged to the same city while serving a distinct gameplay function.

Design Approach
  • Six zones with distinct spatial rhythm, visual language, and gameplay purpose
  • Narrative context embedded in architecture — every area readable as a place with history
  • Custom Unity C# mechanics: climbing system and ledge detection
  • Verticality used as both gameplay tool and narrative metaphor

Hocus Potion

Mobile alchemy game. Writing that sells a world in under 150 characters.

Overview

Hocus Potion is a mobile alchemy game developed as part of the DBGA programme. My contribution focused on app store copywriting — a discipline that demands narrative compression without losing tone or personality.

The Challenge

App store copy lives under extreme constraints: character limits, keyword requirements, platform conventions. The challenge was writing descriptions that felt like invitations into a world rather than feature lists — while still hitting every commercial requirement.

This meant making every word do double duty: communicate tone, communicate function, and hold attention in a context where the reader is already halfway to scrolling past.

What I Learned

Constraint is clarifying. When you can't use ten words, you find the one word that carries everything. That discipline transfers directly to in-game writing — tooltip text, item descriptions, UI copy — anywhere narrative has to work in a small space.

Tides of Vengeance

An original pitch recognised by a lead designer at DBGA. A world built from the sea floor up.

Concept

Tides of Vengeance is an original game concept with a fully developed pitch and worldbuilding documentation. The project was recognised by a lead designer at the academy as a standout original concept — praised for its internal consistency and the distinctiveness of its fictional world.

Worldbuilding Approach

The world of Tides of Vengeance was built around a single guiding question: what does revenge look like when the thing you're avenging no longer exists? Every faction, location, and mechanic was designed to refract that question differently.

Documents Produced
  • Original pitch document — concept, tone, core loop, target audience
  • Worldbuilding documentation — factions, geography, mythology
  • Narrative design notes — protagonist arc, thematic pillars

Culling Circuit

A kart racer built inside the skull of a dead god. Azathoth as finish line.

Concept

Culling Circuit is a cosmic horror kart racing concept blending the energy of Crash Team Racing, the brutality of Mortal Kombat, and the rhythm of Thumper — set inside a mythology where Azathoth's dreaming is the track itself.

Factions race not for glory but for the right to survive the next cycle of the Outer Gods' attention. Losing means ceasing to exist. Winning means you get to race again.

Worldbuilding
  • Cosmological structure built around Lovecraftian mythology — recontextualised for competition
  • Multiple distinct factions with competing eschatologies
  • Visual language: psychedelic, gothic, non-Euclidean track geometry
  • Tone: absurd surface, genuinely unsettling undercurrent

Coming Back

Every thousand years, the god Mucco grants one cow the right to return. You are an hamburger. You are going home.

Overview

Coming Back is a shoot 'em up developed in five days for a game jam on the theme of Origins. The concept: a divine bovine mythology in which the god Mucco periodically resurrects a fallen cow — in the form of a hamburger — and grants them a chance to ascend back through a shoot 'em up gauntlet inspired by Cuphead and Cho Aniki.

The premise is absurdist, but the execution is earnest — which is what makes it work.

My Contribution
  • Original concept and narrative premise
  • All animated background GIFs — produced and integrated in 5 days
  • Original music composition and implementation
  • Creative direction for the team's AI image and visual tool pipeline — no dedicated artist on the team

"No artist on the team. Five days. A playable, visually coherent game. That's what problem-solving under pressure looks like."

Creative Direction Under Constraint

The absence of a dedicated artist forced a fast pivot to AI-assisted visual production — but the outputs still needed creative direction. I developed a prompt documentation workflow on the fly: consistent tone references, style constraints, and iteration notes that let the team generate coherent visuals without a single visual specialist.

This is the most transferable skill to come out of Coming Back: knowing how to maintain aesthetic coherence when the usual pipeline doesn't exist.

Said B.

Narrative Designer · DBGA Milano · First Year

I'm a narrative design student at DBGA in Milan, with a background that refuses to stay in one lane — narrative design, sound design, production, level design, and a long-standing obsession with games that treat their worlds as lived-in places.

My aesthetic preferences run toward the surreal, the gothic, and the genuinely strange. I'm less interested in conventional fantasy settings than in games that find horror in the domestic, grandeur in the mundane, or comedy in the cosmic. Culling Circuit has a dead god as the racetrack. Dream Jumper is a horror game where the monster is memory. Coming Back is about a hamburger ascending to bovine heaven.

Outside of games: I play trumpet, compose music, write weird fiction, do cosplay, and maintain a library of physical games spanning PS1 through PS5. I believe sound design is narrative design. I also believe Mortal Shell deserves more recognition than it gets.

Long-term goal: a narrative or production role at Techland or CD Projekt RED — studios that treat worldbuilding as a craft rather than a checkbox. Currently building the portfolio and the skills to get there by summer 2027.
